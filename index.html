📚 Glossary — click to expand ▾
American Odds
Negative numbers (e.g. -150) show how much you must bet to win $100. Positive numbers (e.g. +130) show how much a $100 bet wins. -110 is the most common line — it's how bookmakers take their cut.
Implied Probability
The win probability baked into the odds by the bookmaker. -110 implies a 52.4% chance of winning. Because both sides add up to more than 100%, the extra is the bookmaker's profit margin (the "vig").
Vig / Juice / Overround
The bookmaker's built-in profit margin. A fair coin flip is 50/50 but a book might price both sides at -110 (52.4% each = 104.8% total). That extra 4.8% is the vig — it means you're paying a tax on every bet.
Expected Value (EV)
The average profit or loss per dollar bet, calculated using the true win probability. A +5% EV bet means you'd expect to make $5 profit for every $100 wagered over many bets. Negative EV means you lose money long-term even if you win sometimes.
Breakeven Win Rate
The minimum percentage of the time you need to win just to avoid losing money. At -110 odds you need to win 52.4% of the time to break even. If you think you'll win more often than that, the bet has value.
Kelly Criterion
A formula that calculates the mathematically optimal stake size based on your edge and bankroll. Betting too little leaves money on the table; betting too much risks ruin. We show ¼ Kelly — a conservative version used by professional bettors.
No-Vig (Fair) Line
The odds with the bookmaker's profit margin removed. If a book has Team A at -110 and Team B at -110, the no-vig line for both sides is -100 (50/50). Comparing where you're betting to the no-vig line tells you how much value you're getting or giving away.
